Latin Plurals for You

Before they all died long, long ago, back when people didn't live long enough to get cancer, the awesome speakers of Latin and Greek left us some kick-ass words. But this is a dangerous gift for us, because the correct plural forms of these words are way too easy for English-speakers to get wrong. Horribly, dreadfully wrong. Kittens-will-die wrong. I wrote this teensy little guide to help people with some of the trickiest ones so that you will stop killing kittens you bastard.

First, I give you two simple but powerful rules:

  1. Do not just go around sticking the letter i on things. Kittens will die.
  2. You're speaking English; you can use English plurals! They're great!

  • Octopus

    Real plural: octopodes (fuck yeah!)
    Recommend: octopuses
    Never say: octopi
    This one comes to us from the Greek okto- (meaning eight) and pous (meaning foot). So it means "eight-foot." It comes out in Latin spelled octopus. The plural is not octopi. Oh no. Instead, it would really be octopodes, which is awesome. If you think octopodes will confuse people too much (which it will, but why not try anyway, just for fun), then it's best just to say "octopuses." Whatever you do, do not, under any circumstances, say octopi. Kittens will die.
    Incidentally that podes form shows the root of the word, which is pod-. The root is used in other compound forms you will recognize, like podiatrist ("foot doctor", from pous and iatros). Note that a "pediatrician" is not related. That's from paid- (root of the word for "child"), so "child doctor". The corresponding Latin word for pous (the actual Latin word for foot) is pes. The Latin root for that is ped-, like pedestrian (someone who goes on foot).

  • Platypus

    Real plural: platypodes
    Recommended: platypuses
    Never say: platypi
    See Octopus for an explanation of the plural. Platy- is from the Greek for "flat," and again, pous means "foot." So a platypus is Greek for "police officer."

  • Virus

    Real plural: virūs (really)
    Recommended: viruses
    Never say: virii (Kittens will die)
    A lot of the words in Latin that end with -us have a plural in -i, but this is not one of them. And it definitely doesn't have a plural in -ii. (I have noticed that people with neck beards are particularly given to using the bogus plural virii.) There are many other words in Latin that sneakily have a singular -us and a plural -ūs. That line over the u means that the vowel is pronounced a little longer, like the a in English "madder" as compared to "matter." In fact, that vowel lengthening is the only reason we can tell those words apart in spoken English, since the medial tt and dd are pronounced exactly the same. The Romans also heard the distinction between long and short vowels. Anyway, the point is that the plural for this word looks exactly the same as the singular (unless you have a fancy macron to put over it's), which is just going to confuse people. Say "viruses."

  • Synopsis

    Real plural: synopses
    Recommended: synopses
    Never say: synopsi
    This is actually a Greek word. It's from syn "together" and opsis "look," so it means a look at things together, or "summary." The real plural is synopses, and since the English pluralization sounds totally whack in this case (synopsises), go with the original Latin. By the way, there are lots of other words that follow this pattern, like crisis/crises and thesis/theses, to name a couple.

  • Cactus

    Real plural: cacti
    Recommended: cactuses
    This is one of those Latin words where people who don't know Latin plurals get it right by accident. Personally, I prefer cactuses as the plural in English, but I can't really argue against people who say cacti and get away with it. Whatever. Suum cuique.

  • Data

    It is actually plural, but treat it as singular
    Say the data shows X.
    Only douchebags say the data show X.
    In Latin, data is the plural of datum, "given." So yes, if we're going to be mindless pedantic twits, we would cry out whenever some barbarian said "the data shows" something or other. But look, the Romans didn't have "data," at least not in the post-18th-century statistical sense that we have data. For us, "data", like a "sample", is a collective; it's a group of things. This word has evolved in modern usage. Let's let it do its thing.
    If you feel like you need some classical linguistic model to back you up at your next cocktail party when some douchebag in a bowtie scuttles up and says, "I wonder whether you realize that data is really plural herp derp derp herp derp," just tell the little guy that, strictly speaking, yes; but it's better construed as a neuter collective, as in the pre-archaic Latin sena, meaning "group of old men," (from the root sen-, meaning "old," as in "senile") whence the Latin Senatus, "Group of senile old white douchebags."

  • Agenda

    It is actually plural, but treat it as singular
    The Latin agenda is the plural of agendum. It means "things to do." Like data, this is a singular word in English already; it denotes a group of stuff treated as a whole. "Do we have an agenda for this meeting?" You can say, as Tom Lehrer does in his song We Will All Go Together When We Go, "We will drop our agendas and adjourn." However, if you act all fancy and say shit like "Amongst the agenda is found an item specifying herp derp derp herp," then people will hit you. You have been warned.

  • Criterion

    Singular: criterion, plural: criteria
    Never say: The criteria is ... or The criterias are ...
    This is a Greek word. Quite a lot of Greek words for a page that claims to be about Latin, aren't there. Well, greekandlatinpluralsforyou.com is too long. Let's move on. So criteria is the plural of criterion. If you suspect that these Greek words ending in -on have something in common with Latin words ending in -um, you are correct! In both languages, the plurals for such nouns end in -a. Labium/labia, etc. Note that unlike data and agenda, criteria doesn't get to be treated as a singular in English. Criterion singular; criteria plural.
