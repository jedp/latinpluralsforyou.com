Latin Plurals for You

Before they all died long, long ago, back in the day when people didn't live long enough to get cancer, the speakers of Latin and Greek left us some kick-ass words to spice up our vocabularies. But they clearly weren't thinking too hard about us, because the plural forms of these words are way too easy for English-speakers to get wrong. Horribly wrong. Dreadfully wrong. Kittens-will-die wrong. I wrote this teensy little guide to help people with some of the trickiest ones so that you will stop killing kittens you bastard.

First, I give you two simple but powerful rules:

  1. If you just start sticking the letter i on things, kittens will die. Seriously.
  2. English plurals are nice when speaking English. Use them and be happy! Octopuses! Viruses! #Winnings!

  • Octopus
    Real plural: octopodes (fuck yeah!)
    Recommend: octopuses
    Never say: octopi
    This one comes to us from the Greek okto- (meaning eight) and pous (meaning foot). So it means "eight-foot." It comes out in Latin as octopus. The plural is not octopi. Instead, it would really be octopodes, which is awesome. But since that will probably confuse people, best just to say "octopuses." Just do not say octopi. Kittens will die if you do.
    That podes form shows the root of the word, which is pod-. The root is used in other compound forms you will recognize, like podiatrist ("foot doctor", from pous and iatros). Note that a "pediatrician" is not related. That's from paid- (root of the word for "child"), so "child doctor".

  • Platypus

    Real plural: platypodes
    Recommended: platypuses
    Never say: platypi
    See octopus for an explanation of the plural. Platy- is from the Greek for "flat," and again, pous means "foot." So a platypus is Greek for "police officer."

  • Virus

    Real plural: virūs
    Recommended: viruses
    Never say: virii (Kittens will die)
    A lot of the words in Latin that end with -us have a plural in -i, but this is not one of them. There are many other words in Latin that sneakily have a singular -us and a plural -ūs. That line over the u means that the vowel is pronounced a little longer, like the a in English "madder" as compared to "matter." In fact, that vowel lengthening is the only reason we can tell those words apart in spoken English, since the medial tt and dd are pronounced exactly the same. The Romans also heard the distinction between long and short vowels. Anyway, the point is that the plural for this word looks exactly the same as the singular (unless you have a fancy macron to put over it), which is just going to confuse people. Say "viruses."

  • Synopsis

    Real plural: synopses
    Recommended: synopses
    Never say: synopsi
    This is actually a Greek word. It's from syn "together" and opsis "look," so it means a look at things together, or "summary." The real plural is synopses, and since the English pluralization sounds totally whack in this case (synopsises), go with the original Latin.

  • Cactus

    Real plural: cacti
    Recommended: cactuses
    This is one of those Latin words where people who don't know Latin plurals get it right by accident. Personally, I prefer cactuses as the plural in English, but I can't really argue against people who say cacti and get away with it. Whatever. Suum cuique.

  • Data

    It is actually plural, but treat it as singular
    Say "the data shows X". Only douchebags say "the data show X."
    In Latin, data is the plural of datum, "given." So yes, if we're going to be mindless pedantic twits, we would cry out whenever some barbarian said "the data shows" something or other. But look, the Romans didn't have "data," at least not in the post-18th-century statistical sense that we have data. For us, "data", like a "sample", is a collective; it's a group of things. This word has evolved in modern usage. Let's let it do its thing. And if you feel like you need some classical linguistic model to back you up at your next cocktail party when some douchebag in a bowtie says, "I wonder whether you realize that data is really plural herp derp derp herp derp," just tell the little guy that it's actually best construed as a neuter collective, as in the pre-archaic Latin sena, meaning "group of old men," (from the root sen-, meaning "old," as in "senile") whence the Latin Senatus, "Group of senile old white douchebags."

  • Agenda

    It is actually plural, but treat it as singular
    Never say: agendas
    The Latin agenda is the plural of agendum. It means "things to do." Like data, this is a singular word in English already; it denotes a group of stuff treated as a whole. "Do we have an agenda for this meeting?" If you act all fancy and say shit like "Amongst the agenda is an item specifying blah," then people will hit you. You have been warned.
